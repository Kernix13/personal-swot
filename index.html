The SWOT analysis is a strategic planning tool used to identify and evaluate the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats related to your career development. It helps you understand your current position and plan for future growth. Strengths and weaknesses are internal factors, while opportunities and threats are external factors.

Questions

There are 4 categories of questions:

  1. S = Strengths
  2. W = Weaknesses
  3. O = Opportunities
  4. T = Threats

Some questions have multiple variations so I put them under a topic heading above the questions. Read them all and then choose ones that you can answer. I used the heading "Other questions" for all other questions.

I did not copy questions that involve co-workers since I am currently unemployed, and I ran my own business for the past 15 years.

From Code:You

Strengths are the positive attributes and skills you possess that can help you achieve your career goals. These might include specific skills, experiences, or personal qualities. For example, being a strong communicator or having a unique technical skill can be considered strengths.

Weaknesses are areas where you may lack skills or experience, which could hinder your career progress. An example of a weakness might be a lack of experience in a particular industry or skill set.

Identifying weaknesses is crucial as it allows you to work on them or find ways to mitigate their impact.

Opportunities in a SWOT analysis refer to external factors that you can leverage to advance your career.

These could include industry trends, networking events, or new job openings. Recognizing opportunities allows you to take proactive steps to capitalize on them.

Threats are external challenges that could negatively impact your career. These might include economic downturns, increased competition, or changes in industry regulations.

Identifying threats helps you prepare and develop strategies to minimize their impact.

By understanding both opportunities and threats, you can better navigate your career path and make informed decisions about your future.

Conducting a SWOT analysis involves a few key steps.

  • First, list your strengths and weaknesses. Be honest and thorough in your assessment.
  • Next, identify opportunities and threats in your external environment. Consider factors such as industry trends, economic conditions, and technological advancements.
  • Once you have your list, analyze how your strengths can help you take advantage of opportunities and mitigate threats.
  • Similarly, consider how you can address your weaknesses to prevent them from hindering your progress.

This analysis will provide you with a comprehensive view of your current career situation and help you develop a strategic plan for growth.

After completing your SWOT analysis, it's important to use the insights gained to set realistic career goals. These goals should leverage your strengths, address your weaknesses, capitalize on opportunities, and prepare for potential threats.

For example, if you identify a strength in project management and an opportunity in a growing industry, you might set a goal to pursue a leadership role in that field. Conversely, if a threat is an economic downturn, you might focus on building a diverse skill set to remain adaptable.

Regularly revisiting and updating your SWOT analysis ensures that you remain aligned with your career objectives and can adapt to changes in your personal and professional environment.

There are 4 steps to this analysis:

1. Create a SWOT matrix - S & W are in the first row of the matrix - these relate to internal factors - O & T in the 2nd row and are external issues or circumstances

Positive Negative
Strengths Weaknesses
Opportunities Threats

1st column: S & O represent the positive or helpful factors

2nd column: W & T represent the negative or harmful issues

2. brainstorm as many considerations as you can under each heading

3. Prioritize the list - work out the top the 3-5 most important factors ie each quadrant

4. analyze the table by looking at each item and considering the actions that you can take - Questions to ask:

  • How can you play to or maximize your strengths?
  • What personal development goals, people or processes could help to address your weaknesses?
  • How can you exploit, expand, or multiply the opportunities?
  • What control measures do you need to put in place to limit the threats?

Now look for further relationships that you can identify across the columns, rows, and diagonals.

Remember weaknesses are often a reflection of strengths - think about the opposite of each strength and weakness you have listed.

Similarly, look at tthe flipside of external factors. You might find that threats can also provide opportunities, e.g. a competitor could be a potential collaborator, a failed project is an opportunity for learning.

Combinations

  • SO (strengths-opportunities): Explore how you can use your strengths to take advantage of opportunities.
  • ST (strengths-threats): Brainstorm how you can implement your strengths to overcome or avoid threats.
  • WO (weaknesses-opportunities): Consider how new opportunities can reduce or resolve your weaknesses.
  • WT (weaknesses-threats): Plan for how you can minimize weaknesses to avoid threats.
